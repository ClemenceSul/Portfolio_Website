CAD Validation

Each piece of the automaton was modelled in Solidworks and assembled to ensure they would function correctly when 3D printed.

Workshop Creation

All elements were 3D printed and melted to be assembled together. A bit of fine tuning was done to smoothen the mechanisms.

Photo / Video

Each mechanism was filmed and smartly edited to display how they function. Sound effects were developed to accentuate each actor’s actions.

Creating the actors. From CAD to life.

Actors are a crutial element when narrating a compelling story. In the factory line, all workers were made identical, with very few details, so the sense of seperate individuals disappears and they become a uniform robot-like group drilling into the ice. In comparison, the animals were given a low poly aesthetic granting them a ghost like appearance as phantoms of a past reality.

Diverse mechanisms. Acting out the scene.

To mimic reality, the cams were designed according to the movements required for each actor. Each worker in the factory line must go up and down to represent drilling. Their cams were designed with a sudden drop to create a constant ticking noise like a machine would. For the penguins, an oscillating motion was preferred, to resemble their walk. The polar bear and seal had irregular cams for a story-like result of the polar bear reaching for the seal. In the polar bear, the weight of the arm pulled the link up, hence an inversed slider was designed.

Final Assembly. A 140 piece puzzle.

Although each part of the automaton can designed separately, all the gears and shafts used the same male / female shapes which made them interchangeable. This was quite useful to test mechanisms with pre-printed pieces and solved multiple issues encountered when assembling. As this project stipulated only 3D printed pieces were allowed, blocks were designed to avoid shift of the pieces on the shafts and others were melted to permanently attach them together.
